UTP — UNSHIELDED TWISTED PAIR

UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair) is the most popular network cable in use today. As the name suggests, it has no shield wrapped around it. Inside, the wires are twisted together in pairs — 4 pairs total (8 wires).

The twisting helps cancel out electromagnetic interference between pairs. Different categories (Cat) of UTP support different speeds, as shown in the table.

UTP At a Glance

  • No shielding (unshielded)
  • 4 twisted pairs = 8 wires
  • Twisting reduces interference
  • Speed depends on Category
  • Most common LAN cable today

// Termination

STRAIGHT-THROUGH vs CROSSOVER

There are normally 2 ways to terminate (wire) a UTP cable, depending on what kind of devices you're connecting.

1 · Straight-Through

Same pinout on both ends

Used to connect different types of devices together — for example, a PC to a Switch, or a Switch to a Router.

2 · Crossover

TX and RX pins swapped

Used to connect the same type of devices together — for example, Switch to Switch, or PC to PC. Note: modern devices with Auto-MDIX can use a straight-through cable even between same-type devices.

Tip: Straight-Through = different device types · Crossover = same device types. Today, Auto-MDIX lets most equipment auto-detect, so a straight-through cable usually works either way.

// Light-Based Cabling

FIBER OPTIC

Fiber optic cable transmits signals using light. Its advantages: it can transmit over very long distances, at high speed, and with no interference problems like UTP.

Single Mode

Long distance

A very thin core that carries a single beam of light in a straight line. Ideal for long-distance connections — across cities or between buildings far apart.

Multi Mode

Short distance

A wider core that carries multiple light beams at different angles. Best for short-distance connections — within a building or data center.

Components of a Fiber Optic Cable

Core

The inner glass core that carries the light signal through the fiber.

Cladding

Also glass — acts as a reflector so light cannot escape the core (total internal reflection).

Coating

A clear plastic layer coating the fiber to protect against moisture, scratches, and impact.

Loose Tube

A loose, durable tube that protects the optical fibers from impact and pulling tension.

Central Strength Member

The cable's core made of strong metal/insulator rod that bears the cable's weight and external forces.

Jacket (Sheath)

The outermost shell — Single Jacket or Double Jacket (Outer + Inner) for better impact protection.

// Connecting Fiber

SFP MODULE & LC CONNECTOR

To connect Fiber Optic to a network device or Switch, you need an SFP (Small Form-Factor Pluggable) module. Its job is to convert between light signals and electrical signals.

The connector head on the fiber cable itself is called an LC connector — short for Little Connector.

SFP = Signal Converter LC = Little Connector Light ⇄ Electrical
